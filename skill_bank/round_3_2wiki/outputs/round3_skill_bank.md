# Retrieval Skill Bank B3

`single-entity-relation-lookup`
Use when the question already names one resolved target entity, event, work, or concept and asks for one direct relation or attribute. Query the exact target name plus the relation phrase, prefer a canonical page or infobox if available, and verify that the retrieved sentence explicitly links that target to the asked attribute before extracting the answer. Do not use when the final target must be found through another entity or clue chain, when multiple candidates must be compared or filtered, when the task is mainly name-form resolution, or when a yes-no answer depends on combining evidence from more than one page.

`surface-name-resolution`
Use when the question asks for a real name, full name, alternate name, former name, nickname, stage name, or renamed identity of a mention. Search the mention with its surrounding context and name-linking cues such as real name, full name, also known as, formerly, or, for character-to-actor phrasing, portrayed by, then verify that the evidence explicitly maps the asked name form to the returned name. Do not use for generic role, biography, or attribute questions that do not ask to resolve one name form into another.

`multi-constraint-query-anchoring`
Use when the question is long or clue-heavy, or when the target is described by a distinctive phrase instead of a short canonical name, especially to stabilize hop one of a later bridge search. Build the query from 2 to 4 rare anchors spanning the key constraints, keep exact titles or unusual phrases intact, and verify that the chosen passage satisfies every major constraint before trusting it as the target or bridge. Do not use as a substitute for a true two-hop plan once the intermediate entity has been found, and do not use for short direct lookups or explicit pairwise comparison questions.

`superlative-ranking-match`
Use when the question asks for the first, largest, smallest, highest, lowest, top-ranked, oldest, or similar superlative within an explicit set or metric. Search with the comparator, the measure, the comparison set, and any time qualifier together, and verify that the evidence states the same ranking for the same domain and timeframe before answering. Do not use when the question asks for the raw numeric value itself or when only two named entities need a direct pairwise comparison.

`forced-choice-option-resolution`
Use when one subject must be resolved among a small set of explicit options, such as state or territory, city or country, AC or DC, or tomb or palace. Search the subject with the disputed property and the option terms, then verify that evidence explicitly classifies the subject into one option or gives a more specific fact that unambiguously resolves the choice. Do not use for open-ended wh-questions, broad yes-no questions without explicit alternatives, or pairwise entity comparisons.

`bridge-entity-search`
Use when the answer is an attribute of one hidden intermediate entity linked from a named entity by a single bridge clue, and the question has only one such branch to resolve. First search for the bridge entity using the clue that links the named entity to another page, such as played for, voice actor, featuring, written by, located in, spouse of, or father of; then run a second search for the asked attribute of that confirmed bridge. Verify hop one with an explicit relation and verify hop two on the same bridged target before answering. Do not use when the question spells out a cleaner edge-by-edge relation chain, when two sides must each be bridged before comparison or verification, or when the chain has multiple intermediate checkpoints that require sequential-hop-checkpointing.

`bridge-disambiguate-then-hop`
Use when hop one in a one-branch bridge question produces several plausible people, works, organizations, episodes, or pages because the clue is alias-heavy, dense with entities, or missing a unique title. Search hop one with 2 to 4 rare anchors from the clue bundle, resolve the bridge candidate with the strongest leftover clue before launching hop two, then search the asked attribute only on that confirmed bridge. Verify both that the chosen bridge matches all key clues and that hop two explicitly states the final relation. Do not use when the bridge entity is already uniquely named, when both sides of a later comparison must each be resolved, or when the task mainly needs sequential checkpointing across three or more edges rather than one difficult hop-one disambiguation.

`relation-chain-decomposition`
Use when the question states an explicit ordered chain of relations, such as director of X, father of Y, performer of song Z, or mother of the person who, so the search should follow one named edge at a time. Search hop one for the first relation to identify the intermediate entity, then search each next relation or endpoint attribute from the immediately confirmed intermediate page, keeping relation labels separate instead of collapsing all clues into one query. Verify every edge explicitly and only answer when the final entity or value is supported through that same ordered chain. Do not use for loose descriptive bridges with no clear named relations, for derived kinship roles like mother-in-law or paternal grandmother that require an inference join over family structure, or for parallel two-side comparisons where each branch must be solved separately.

`bridge-comparison-planning`
Use when the question compares or verifies two named subjects, but each side first requires a hidden intermediate entity before the compared attribute can be read, such as film → director → birth date or film → director → country. Resolve the bridge on side A and side B separately with matched relation labels, then retrieve the final attribute for each resolved intermediate before making the comparison or yes-no decision. Verify both bridge edges and both endpoint attributes under the same definition, unit, and timeframe before answering. Do not use for direct two-entity comparisons where no hidden intermediate is needed, for ordinary one-sided bridge questions, or for short relation chains that end in a single extracted answer rather than a cross-side comparison.

`derived-kinship-inference-join`
Use when the asked relation is a derived family role that is usually not stated directly on one page, such as paternal grandmother, maternal grandfather, mother-in-law, or father-in-law. Search first for the anchor person's immediate relative that determines the family side or marriage link, then search that intermediate page for the requested parent or spouse relation needed to derive the final kinship. Verify the intermediate link and the second family edge explicitly, making sure side modifiers like paternal versus maternal or in-law versus blood relation are preserved. Do not use for direct parent, spouse, child, or sibling lookups that are stated in one hop, or for non-family bridge questions where the relation chain is already explicitly spelled out.

`sequential-hop-checkpointing`
Use when solving the question requires more than one intermediate checkpoint, such as three-plus edges in one chain or two mirrored two-hop branches before comparison, and collapsing the query risks skipping hidden steps. Retrieve one edge at a time, record each confirmed intermediate entity, and launch the next search only from the currently verified checkpoint rather than from the full original question. Verify every hop locally before advancing and verify the final answer against the full reconstructed chain or both reconstructed branches. Do not use for ordinary two-hop bridges or explicit one-intermediate relation chains where a simpler bridge or relation-chain skill already fits cleanly.

`parallel-attribute-compare`
Use when two named entities must be checked on the same directly retrievable attribute, including which is earlier or larger, whether both fit a category, or what attribute they share, and no hidden intermediate is needed for either side. Retrieve matched evidence for each side using the same relation label, definition, unit, and timeframe; if one side only supplies the relation cue, use it to identify the attribute type and then look up the other side under that same attribute. Verify that the comparison is like-for-like before deciding or extracting the shared attribute. Do not use for one-subject bridge questions, for questions where either side first requires resolving a hidden intermediate such as film → director, or for yes-no questions whose final truth depends on a multi-branch claim after bridges are collected; use bridge-comparison-planning or multihop-yes-no-verification there.

`multihop-yes-no-verification`
Use for yes-no questions whose truth depends on combining two or more retrieved propositions after the needed entities and attributes have been gathered, such as same-country checks, founder-of claims, membership claims, or relational comparisons after multihop expansion. Gather each required proposition separately, normalize names or comparable values if needed, and answer yes only when every component is explicitly satisfied under the same definition and timeframe; answer no when any required component is directly contradicted or fails after matched evidence is collected. Verify that the verdict is based on the full set of required facts, not on one supportive snippet, and treat this as the final verification layer rather than as the hop-discovery plan itself. Do not use for open-ended wh-questions, simple one-page yes-no lookups, or as a substitute for bridge-comparison-planning, relation-chain-decomposition, or bridge-entity-search when the hidden entities still need to be found.

`temporal-range-extract`
Use when the final answer to extract is a date, year, age, duration, count, population, measurement, or bounded range for an already identified target, including after a completed multihop chain. Search with the resolved target plus the requested metric and any supplied time context, then verify that the value matches the same unit, scope, and timeframe in evidence before extracting it. If the question first requires a bridge, explicit relation chain, or longer checkpointed path, finish that targeting step before running this extraction. Do not use when the task asks which entity ranks first, largest, smallest, earlier, or later within a set instead of asking for the raw value itself.

`conflict-check`
Use when retrieval surfaces multiple plausible entities, titles, organizations, dates, or numbers at either hop of a question. Run one targeted disambiguation search anchored on the current candidate plus the asked relation and the question's strongest leftover clue; in multihop cases, resolve hop-one ambiguity before trusting hop two, or resolve hop-two ambiguity before answering. Keep a candidate only if that relation is explicitly confirmed for the exact entity under consideration. Do not use as a default extra search when evidence already agrees, or as a replacement for a full bridge or comparison plan.

`verbatim-evidence-span`
Use right before answering when the evidence contains the answer as a clean span, especially for names, titles, aliases, quoted phrases, units, or short lists. Copy the shortest faithful span that is explicitly tied to the asked relation, preserving capitalization, quotation marks, units, and list structure that disambiguate the answer. Do not use to invent a normalization that is not explicitly supported by the retrieved text.

`answer-grounding-check`
Use after drafting an answer to confirm that the current evidence explicitly supports both the candidate and the asked relation, and for multihop questions confirm that the bridge or comparison links are also explicitly supported. If the support is only implicit, off-target, from a near-match entity, or missing one hop in the chain, do one extra targeted search anchored on the draft answer and the unresolved relation before finalizing. Do not skip this check when the answer was inferred from multiple passages or when routing passed through a bridge, comparison, or verification skill.
