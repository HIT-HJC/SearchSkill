# Retrieval Skill Bank final

`single-entity-relation-lookup`
Use when the question already names one resolved target entity, event, work, or concept and asks for one direct relation or attribute. Query the exact target name plus the relation phrase, prefer a canonical page or infobox if available, and verify that the retrieved sentence explicitly links that target to the asked attribute before extracting the answer. Do not use when the final target must be found through another entity or clue chain, when multiple candidates must be compared or filtered, when the task is mainly name-form resolution, or when a yes-no answer depends on combining evidence from more than one page.

`surface-name-resolution`
Use when the question asks for a real name, full name, alternate name, former name, nickname, stage name, or renamed identity of a mention. Search the mention with its surrounding context and name-linking cues such as real name, full name, also known as, formerly, or, for character-to-actor phrasing, portrayed by, then verify that the evidence explicitly maps the asked name form to the returned name. Do not use for generic role, biography, or attribute questions that do not ask to resolve one name form into another.

`multi-constraint-query-anchoring`
Use when the question is long, clue-heavy, or alias-prone and the next target is described by several constraints instead of a short canonical name, especially at hop one of a later bridge or long-hop plan. Build the query from 2 to 4 rare anchors spanning the key constraints, keep exact titles or unusual phrases intact, and choose a candidate only if the passage satisfies every major constraint needed for the next hop. Verify the anchored candidate before continuing, then drop consumed clues and re-anchor on the new checkpoint rather than repeatedly searching the full question. Do not use as a substitute for a true multihop plan once the intermediate has been found, and do not use for short direct lookups, simple one-bridge questions, or explicit relation chains where edge-by-edge decomposition is cleaner.

`superlative-ranking-match`
Use when the question asks for the first, largest, smallest, highest, lowest, top-ranked, oldest, or similar superlative within an explicit set or metric. Search with the comparator, the measure, the comparison set, and any time qualifier together, and verify that the evidence states the same ranking for the same domain and timeframe before answering. Do not use when the question asks for the raw numeric value itself or when only two named entities need a direct pairwise comparison.

`forced-choice-option-resolution`
Use when the question gives a small explicit option set and the final answer must be one of those options, such as state or territory, city or country, AC or DC, tomb or palace, or A or B. Treat the option strings in the question as answer anchors: search the subject plus the disputed property and option terms, verify evidence that explicitly classifies the subject into exactly one option or gives a specific fact that unambiguously maps to one option, then answer with the option span from the question rather than a third entity or a yes/no verdict. If both options require evidence, check both under the same relation before choosing. Do not use for open-ended wh-questions, broad yes-no questions without explicit alternatives, or comparisons where the requested answer is a value rather than one of the stated options.

`bridge-entity-search`
Use when the answer is an attribute of one hidden intermediate entity linked from a named entity by a single bridge clue, and the question has only one branch and only one checkpoint before extraction. First search for the bridge entity using the clue that links the named entity to another page, such as played for, voice actor, featuring, written by, located in, spouse of, or headquarters in; then run a second search for the asked attribute of that confirmed bridge. Verify hop one with an explicit relation, verify hop two on the same bridged target, and preserve answer type when extracting dates, numbers, or spans. Do not use when the chain has two or more hidden checkpoints, when the question is clearer as an explicit ordered relation chain, or when both sides of a comparison must each be bridged before verification.

`bridge-disambiguate-then-hop`
Use when a one-branch two-hop question still has a messy hop one because the bridge candidate is alias-heavy, dense with entities, or underspecified by title. Search hop one with 2 to 4 rare anchors from the clue bundle, resolve the bridge candidate with the strongest leftover clue before launching hop two, then search the asked attribute only on that confirmed bridge. Verify both that the chosen bridge matches all key clues and that hop two explicitly states the final relation. Do not use when the bridge entity is already uniquely named, when there are three or more checkpoints to preserve, or when ambiguity appears only after a later hop and should instead be handled by conflict-check or reconstructed-chain-verification.

`relation-chain-decomposition`
Use when the question states an explicit ordered chain of named relations, such as director of X, father of Y, performer in Z, county of the city where, or state where a film takes place, so the search should follow one labeled edge at a time. Search hop one for the first relation to identify the intermediate entity, then search each next relation or endpoint attribute from the immediately confirmed checkpoint, keeping relation labels separate instead of collapsing all clues into one query. Verify every edge explicitly, preserve modifiers like season, edition, paternal, former, or located in, and answer only when the final entity or value is supported through that same ordered chain. Do not use for loose descriptive bridges with no clear named relations, for derived kinship roles that require an inference join over family structure, or for long clue-heavy paths where the next hop must be re-anchored from leftover context rather than from a clean relation label.

`bridge-comparison-planning`
Use when the question compares or verifies two named subjects, but each side first requires a hidden intermediate entity before the compared attribute can be read, such as film to director to birth date or team to city to rainfall record. Resolve the bridge on side A and side B separately with matched relation labels, then retrieve the final attribute for each resolved intermediate before making the comparison, selection, or yes-no decision. Verify both bridge edges and both endpoint attributes under the same definition, unit, and timeframe before answering. Do not use for direct two-entity comparisons with no hidden intermediate, for one-sided bridge questions, or for longer 3-plus-hop branches that need sequential-hop-checkpointing or re-anchored-long-hop-decomposition before any comparison can be trusted.

`derived-kinship-inference-join`
Use when the asked relation is a derived family role that is usually not stated directly on one page, such as paternal grandmother, maternal grandfather, mother-in-law, or father-in-law. Search first for the anchor person's immediate relative that determines the family side or marriage link, then search that intermediate page for the requested parent or spouse relation needed to derive the final kinship. Verify the intermediate link and the second family edge explicitly, making sure side modifiers like paternal versus maternal or in-law versus blood relation are preserved. Do not use for direct parent, spouse, child, or sibling lookups that are stated in one hop, or for non-family bridge questions where the relation chain is already explicitly spelled out.

`sequential-hop-checkpointing`
Use when solving the question requires more than one intermediate checkpoint, such as a 3-plus-edge chain or two mirrored multi-step branches, and collapsing the query risks skipping hidden steps or drifting to a near-match. Retrieve one edge at a time, record each confirmed intermediate entity as a checkpoint, and launch the next search only from the current verified checkpoint plus the unresolved remainder of the question rather than from the entire original wording. Verify every hop locally before advancing, and if a later hop fails, restart from the last secure checkpoint instead of from scratch. Do not use for ordinary one-intermediate bridges, simple explicit two-hop chains, or direct comparisons where each side already exposes the compared attribute without hidden steps.

`re-anchored-long-hop-decomposition`
Use when a clue-heavy question hides a 3-hop or 4-hop path but does not cleanly name every intermediate up front, so each found entity must become the new anchor for the next search. Search one hop at a time, carrying forward only the unresolved remainder of the question plus the current verified checkpoint instead of the full original wording, and re-anchor after each hop before launching the next. Verify every checkpoint with the local relation that produced it, then verify that the final endpoint is extracted from the last confirmed intermediate rather than from a globally similar page. Do not use for direct lookups, ordinary two-hop bridges, or explicit edge-by-edge chains that already fit relation-chain-decomposition cleanly.

`parallel-attribute-compare`
Use when two named entities must be checked on the same directly retrievable attribute, including which is earlier or larger, whether both fit a category, or what attribute they share, and no hidden intermediate is needed for either side. Retrieve matched evidence for each side using the same relation label, definition, unit, and timeframe; if one side only supplies the relation cue, use it to identify the attribute type and then look up the other side under that same attribute. Verify that the comparison is like-for-like before deciding or extracting the shared attribute. Do not use for one-subject bridge questions, for questions where either side first requires resolving a hidden intermediate such as film to director, or for yes-no questions whose final truth depends on a multi-branch claim after bridges are collected; use bridge-comparison-planning or multihop-yes-no-verification there.

`multihop-yes-no-verification`
Use only when the question's required final answer is yes or no and the verdict depends on combining two or more retrieved propositions after the needed entities and attributes have been gathered, such as same-country checks, founder-of claims, membership claims, or relational comparisons after multihop expansion. Decompose the claim into required subclaims, gather matched evidence for each subclaim separately, normalize names or comparable values only after retrieval, and answer yes only when every required subclaim is explicitly satisfied under the same definition and timeframe. Answer no when any required subclaim is contradicted or remains unsupported after matched evidence is collected. The final answer under this skill must be exactly yes or no; do not output an entity, title, date, or option span. Do not use for open-ended wh-questions, simple one-page yes-no lookups, or before the hidden entities, branches, or checkpoints have been resolved by bridge, chain, or sequential-hop skills.

`temporal-anchor-carry-forward`
Use when a year, date, season, last-time, before-after, or other temporal qualifier constrains an intermediate relation in a multihop chain, not just the final answer type. Attach the temporal anchor to the hop it modifies, search that hop with the time phrase and entity together, and keep that resolved time-scoped result as the checkpoint before continuing downstream. Verify that the retrieved intermediate is correct under the same timeframe, then verify the final endpoint on the entity produced by that time-scoped hop. Do not use when time is only the final attribute to extract after the chain is already resolved, or when a single-page lookup already states the answer directly.

`temporal-range-extract`
Use when the final answer to extract is a date, year, age, duration, count, population, measurement, rank, or bounded range for an already identified target, including after a completed multihop chain. Search with the resolved target plus the requested metric and any supplied time context, then verify that the value matches the same unit, scope, rank definition, and timeframe in evidence before extracting it. If the question first requires a bridge, explicit relation chain, or longer checkpointed path, finish that targeting step before running this extraction, and prefer the minimal verbatim span that preserves units or date granularity. Do not use when the task is to discover the target entity itself, when a superlative within a set must still be computed, or when a temporal qualifier belongs to an earlier hop and should instead be handled by temporal-anchor-carry-forward.

`conflict-check`
Use when retrieval surfaces multiple plausible entities, titles, organizations, dates, or numbers at the current hop, or when a later result seems inconsistent with an earlier checkpoint. Run one targeted disambiguation search anchored on the current candidate plus the asked relation and the question's strongest leftover clue; in multihop cases, resolve hop-one ambiguity before trusting hop two, and if hop-k conflicts with earlier context, back up to the last secure checkpoint and re-search from there. Keep a candidate only if that relation is explicitly confirmed for the exact entity under consideration. Do not use as a default extra search when evidence already agrees, and do not substitute it for a full bridge, chain, or sequential-hop plan.

`reconstructed-chain-verification`
Use as the final stop-check for 3-plus-hop questions, or for any long chain where the endpoint could be read from a wrong but nearby entity after drift. Before answering, replay the confirmed checkpoints in order, make sure each intermediate still satisfies the downstream clue that depended on it, and confirm that the extracted answer is attached to the final intended target under the asked type, unit, and timeframe. If any hop lacks explicit support or the endpoint could also fit a sibling candidate, run one targeted search from the last secure checkpoint rather than trusting the draft answer. Do not use for short direct lookups or simple two-hop questions whose local hop verification already fully grounds the answer.

`verbatim-evidence-span`
Use right before answering when the evidence contains the answer as a clean span, especially for names, titles, aliases, quoted phrases, units, or short lists. Copy the shortest faithful span that is explicitly tied to the asked relation, preserving capitalization, quotation marks, units, and list structure that disambiguate the answer. Do not use to invent a normalization that is not explicitly supported by the retrieved text.

`answer-grounding-check`
Use immediately before any final answer. Run a strict grounding checklist: the candidate answer must appear in or be directly entailed by retrieved evidence; the evidence must attach that candidate to the exact relation asked by the question; and the answer type must match the question type. For bridge or chain questions, replay each checkpoint from the original anchor to the final endpoint and verify every edge before extracting the endpoint answer. For comparisons, verify both sides with the same relation, unit, definition, and timeframe before choosing. For forced-choice questions, the final answer must be one explicit option from the question. For yes-no questions, the final answer must be exactly yes or no and must follow from all required subclaims, not one supportive snippet. For numeric or temporal answers, confirm unit, granularity, and timeframe; for span answers, prefer the shortest faithful span tied to the asked relation. If support is implicit, off-target, from a near-match entity, copied from a question anchor, missing a hop, or mismatched in answer type, do one targeted search from the last secure checkpoint plus the unresolved relation before finalizing. Do not use this as an extra-search habit when evidence is already sufficient, and do not treat it as a substitute for reconstructed-chain-verification on long 3-plus-hop questions.
